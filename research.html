Diagnosing Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD) and Systemic Sclerosis (SSc) Using Machine Learning

Duration: May 2023 - July2023

Institution: Northwestern University's Winter Lab of Functional Genomics

In early stages of GERD and SSc, symptoms are similar, potentially causing one to be mistaken for the other when diagnosing a patient. Using single-cell data from 33 patients, we focused on learning more about each disease and learning how to use machine learning (ML) to diagnose them. First, we pre-processed data and generated elementary visualizations. We performed an exploratory data analysis composed of univariate and bivariate analysis. After performing dimensionaity reduction using t-SNE and PCA, we tested 4 ML models: K-Nearest Neighbors (kNN), Support Vector Machine (SVM), Logsitic Regression, and Random Forest. Logistic Regression achieved the highest accuracy of 78%.

Advancing Diabetic Retinopathy Diagnosis: A Deep Learning Approach Using Vision Transformer Models

Duration: August 2023 - May 2024

Institution: Personal Research Project (No Affiliated Institution)

Abstract: Diabetic Retinopathy (DR), a severe eye complication associated with diabetes, leads to blindness in over 10,000 individuals annually. Early diagnosis is critical for preventing significant vision loss in patients. In this study, we employ a vision transformer model (ViT) on the RetinaMNIST dataset. Our results indicate that the ViT model surpasses previous baseline models in both accuracy and Area Under the ROC Curve (AUROC) on the RetinaMNIST dataset. The prior leading model achieved an accuracy of 0.531 and an AUROC of 0.750 on a held-out evaluation dataset. Whereas, our ViT model attained an accuracy of 0.567 and an AUROC of 0.773. Limited access to high-quality fundus imaging and ensuring patient saftey poses a large challenge for clinical implementation.

Enhancing Fake News Detection Using BERT and Support Vector Machines on the LIAR Dataset

Duration: May 2024 - July 2024

Institution: MIT Lincoln Laboratories

Abstract: Fake news detection has emerged as a critical task in today’s digital era where misinformation is rapidly spreading. This study explores the application of a Support Vector Machine (SVM) to classify fake news on a 6-level gradient of truth using the LIAR dataset, a comprehensive collection of labeled political news statements. We utilized pre-trained BERT to extract feature representations of the textual data, and then passed these representations into a variety of machine learning models/algorithms including Naïve Bayes, Logistic Regression, Random Trees, and SVM for fake news classification. We found SVM scored the highest accuracy of 26%, replicating the results reported in the original paper introducing the LIAR dataset. This outcome demonstrates the inherent challenges in fake news detection due to the nuanced and often context-dependent nature of misinformation. These findings highlight that pre-trained BERT is insufficient for successful fake news detection and more advanced language models are required to improve performance in this domain.
